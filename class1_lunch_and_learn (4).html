    Act I · The Family Tree

    AI is a family of related ideas, not one thing.

    The class opened by zooming in layer by layer. Each term below is more specific than the one before it. This mental model makes the whole topic less blurry.

    The instructor's framing that stuck with me: "GenAI is very, very, very powerful auto-complete." Not a dismissal — a useful anchor.

    Key insight from class

    AI has been around since the 1950s. What changed recently was scale — more data, more compute, and a 2017 architecture breakthrough called the Transformer.

    Common misconception

    LLMs don't "understand" language. They find statistical patterns in trillions of words and predict what comes next. Fluent output does not equal verified truth.

    Artificial Intelligence

    Any technique enabling computers to mimic human behavior — from chess programs to chatbots to self-driving cars. It's the broadest category. Narrow AI (like ChatGPT) handles one context. General AI and Super AI don't exist yet.

    Act II · How Machines Learn

    There are four ways a model can learn.

    One of the most practical parts of the class. Each approach suits a different kind of problem — and knowing which is which helps you ask better questions about any AI tool.

    1 · Supervised Learning

    Trains on labeled examples (input + correct answer). The model learns to map inputs to outputs.

    → Email spam filters · X-ray tumor detection · Price prediction

    2 · Unsupervised Learning

    Finds hidden structure in unlabeled data. The model invents its own categories.

    → Customer segmentation · Anomaly detection · Topic clustering

    3 · Semi-Supervised Learning

    A small amount of labeled data guides pattern discovery in a much larger unlabeled set.

    → Fraud detection · Network intrusion · Medical imaging

    4 · Reinforcement Learning

    An agent learns by trial and error — rewarded for correct moves, penalized for wrong ones.

    → AlphaGo · Self-driving cars · Training ChatGPT (RLHF)

    The model doesn't "know" you

    It finds statistical patterns in past behavior and projects them forward. It has no idea what you actually value — only what you clicked.

    Data quality checklist (from class)

    A. Can it be used? (available, accessible)
    B. Should it be used? (privacy, laws, regulations)
    C. Is it high quality? (relevant, recent, unbiased)

    Act III · Inside an LLM

    LLMs predict what comes next, one token at a time.

    An LLM is not retrieving a stored answer from a database. It's doing probability-weighted next-token prediction based on patterns from trillions of words of training data.

    The key 2017 breakthrough was the Transformer — a neural network that can compare all parts of a sentence simultaneously (not word by word), enabling it to understand long-range relationships in text.

    1943Perceptron invented — the first artificial neuron, modeled on the brain
    1986Backpropagation unlocks real neural network training (Geoffrey Hinton)
    2006Hinton coins "Deep Learning" — computers learn to recognize image patterns
    2017"Attention is all you need" — the Transformer architecture is born at Google
    2020GPT-3: 175 billion parameters, trained on ~10 trillion words
    2022ChatGPT launches publicly — 1 million users in 5 days
    Hallucination is structural, not a bug

    Because LLMs generate the statistically likely next token, they can produce fluent, confident-sounding text that is factually wrong. Always verify important claims from an AI output.

    Next-token prediction demo

    Type a phrase and see what the model thinks is statistically likely to come next.

    What this shows

    The model isn't choosing the "true" next word. It's ranking statistically likely continuations based on patterns from training data — nothing more.

    📚 From class: GPT-3 trained on ~10 trillion tokens using 10,000 Nvidia V100 GPUs. Estimated training cost for GPT-4: $50–100M+.

    Act IV · Making LLMs Useful at Work

    Raw LLMs need context, tools, and guardrails.

    A bare LLM only knows what was in its training data, which has a cutoff date and no access to your organization's documents or current policies. These techniques close that gap.

    RAG · Retrieval-Augmented Generation

    Before generating a response, the system searches a trusted knowledge base (your documents, policies, databases) and feeds relevant results to the LLM as context.

    → Reduces hallucinations · No retraining needed · Cites sources · Works with internal docs

    Fine-Tuning

    Continues training an existing LLM on your domain-specific data so it bakes in your vocabulary, policies, and style.

    → More consistent responses · Expensive · Static — doesn't auto-update when policies change

    Microsoft 365 Copilot

    ChatGPT embedded in Word, Outlook, Teams, Excel, and PowerPoint — connected to your Microsoft Graph data via RAG.

    → Draft emails · Summarize threads · Analyze spreadsheet data · Generate slides from a Word doc

    Answer Engines (Perplexity, Google AI Mode)

    Unlike a search engine (gives links) or base ChatGPT (uses training data only), answer engines retrieve live sources and synthesize a cited response.

    → Research and fact-checking · Up-to-date stats · Not ideal for creative writing or coding

    The agent loop — AI that keeps working

    The class also introduced agents: AI systems that don't just answer once but cycle through steps until a goal is reached or a human review point is hit.

    1
    Look

    Read the request and gather what's needed.

    2
    Decide

    Choose the next useful step toward the goal.

    3
    Do

    Use a tool, search, draft, summarize, or update.

    4
    Check

    Review the result — stop, revise, or continue.

    Hands-On Activity · Try It Yourself

    You're about to train an AI — right now.

    Google's Quick, Draw! is a neural network that guesses what you're drawing. Every sketch you submit becomes a labeled training example that makes the model smarter for the next person. Over 50 million people have contributed more than 1 billion drawings.

    This is supervised learning happening in real time — you're the labeler, and the model is updating from your data.

    ✏️

    Quick, Draw!

    You'll have 20 seconds to draw each prompt. The AI guesses as you draw — watch how it reasons through incomplete information.

    supervised learning crowdsourced labels neural network live training data

    Opens in a new tab — come back and discuss after a round!

    What to notice while you play

    The AI guesses before you finish

    It's doing classification on incomplete data — the same way a model might flag a suspicious transaction before a full pattern emerges. It's not waiting for certainty.

    Your drawing becomes training data

    When the AI guesses correctly and you confirm, that sketch gets labeled and folded into the dataset. You just contributed to a supervised learning model — exactly what the class described.

    It doesn't "see" like you do

    The model learned from millions of labeled sketches. It's matching pixel patterns to known categories — not understanding what a cat or a bicycle "means." A slightly unusual drawing can completely fool it.

    📚 The Quick, Draw! dataset is publicly available. Researchers use it to study how humans draw, how neural networks generalize, and how cultural differences affect sketching patterns across 15+ languages.

    Discussion question for the table

    If the AI gets your drawing wrong — whose "fault" is it? Yours for drawing differently than most people? Or the training data for not including your style? What does that mean for AI systems we use at work?

    What Prompt Engineering actually is

    Writing a prompt is the only control you have over an LLM's output. The model is fixed — you can't retrain it on the fly. Your prompt is the steering wheel. Next session covers the patterns that consistently produce better results: being specific about role, context, task, and format; using examples; and knowing when to chain steps instead of asking everything at once.

    Common assumption to challenge

    "Better prompts are just longer prompts." Not true. A focused, structured short prompt almost always outperforms a rambling long one. We'll explore why — and practice the patterns that actually move the needle.
